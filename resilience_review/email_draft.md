**1. Adaptation Strategies Narrative (1 page)**
`resilience_review/adaptation_strategies_narrative.md`

Anchored in the MIRR's own bottom-line finding: *"no immediate risk to mission other than single points of failure at each installation."* The narrative covers all four installations with near-term strategies (0–5 years) and long-term strategies (5–20 years). Near-term priorities include expanding JEA IGSAs at NAS JAX and NS Mayport to include resilience provisions (MIRR Regional Recommendation R-2), accelerating the in-progress JEA microgrid feasibility study at NAS JAX, ensuring the funded P035 Dual Fuel Generator Project at Blount Island reaches construction without delay, hardening the JEA Mayport Substation against storm surge (the highest-urgency energy risk in the region), and establishing FPL baseline coordination for Camp Blanding. Long-term strategies focus on breaking the single-substation dependency at all four installations through a regional utility coordination framework, dedicated redundant supply paths, and renewable/microgrid development at Camp Blanding.

**2. Energy Risk Map (Interactive HTML)**
`resilience_review/energy_risk_map.html`

Open in any browser. Five toggleable layers, all grounded in MIRR VA data:
- Four installations with confirmed energy risk ratings and mission summaries
- Four confirmed energy SPOFs (substations) with risk-to-mission ratings from the Jan 2026 SC
- Hazard exposure zones (SLOSH Cat 3–5 surge, CDBG rainfall flood, USDA wildfire WUI) from MIRR data sources
- Existing resilience projects (P035, JEA microgrid study, shoreline restoration)
- MIRR Adaptation Priority sites tied to Regional Recommendation R-2

**3. Strategy Evaluation Questions Analysis**
`resilience_review/evaluation_questions_analysis.md`

Evaluates both the MIRR 8-step Adaptation Planning Approach and the five TAC Discussion Questions from the February 2026 TAC meeting for applicability to energy infrastructure. Question 4 ("Beyond O&M, are there active discussions with utilities on resilience or redundancy — at project or system scale?") is the most directly applicable and is recommended to be retained as-is with a follow-on question about elevating to system-scale coordination. Key recommended modifications to other questions: add utility restoration access routing to Q1, name utilities and resilience scope explicitly in Q3, and add substation flood co-benefit framing to Q5. For the 8-step approach, recommends adding energy-specific quantitative metrics to Step 7 (backup duration, islanding capability, fuel supply lead time).

**4. Energy Reliability Evaluation Methodology**
`resilience_review/evaluation_methodology.md`

Reproducible methodology using the MIRR's confirmed data sources: NEFRC CDBG flood models, NHC SLOSH + Resilient Jax for surge, NOAA/Resilient Jax 2022 SLR projections, Resilient Jax compound flood (2070), USDA Forest Service wildfire, North Florida MPO transportation network, Trust for Public Land heat severity, NWS Jacksonville wind, plus the energy-specific datasets from our prior dataset inventory (EIA Form 860, HIFLD, OpenEnergyHub, FEMA NFHL, USGS, NOAA SLR Calculator, natural gas pipelines). Includes a structured data gap table with five critical gaps confirmed through the MIRR TAC process: JEA utility infrastructure data (follow-ups still being scheduled as of Sept 2025 TAC), NS Mayport detailed data, Camp Blanding data request, CCUA, and on-installation asset data.
