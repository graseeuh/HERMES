# Energy Reliability Adaptation Strategies
## Northeast Florida Military Installation Resilience Review (NEFRC MIRR)
**Naval Air Station Jacksonville | Naval Station Mayport | MCSF Blount Island | Camp Blanding JTF**
**Duval County and Clay County, Florida**

---

The NEFRC Military Installation Readiness Review vulnerability assessment reached a critical conclusion regarding energy infrastructure: *there is no immediate risk to mission at the regional level other than single points of failure at each installation.* This finding sharpens the adaptation challenge — the priority is not a broad systemic overhaul, but targeted elimination of the four confirmed electrical SPOFs before a hazard event exposes their consequences. All four installations rely on a single electrical substation with no backup supply path, and existing utility Intergovernmental Service Agreements (IGSAs) with JEA and FPL cover only operations and maintenance, not resilience or redundancy.

**Installation-Level Risk Summary.** At Naval Station Mayport, the JEA Mayport Substation — located south of the installation — faces storm surge and rainfall flooding exposure and carries a **High** risk-to-mission designation at **Immediate** urgency. NS Mayport hosts the 4th Fleet, UAV operations, regional fuel depots, and major ship repair; a sustained power outage during or after a surge event would directly interrupt primary mission operations. At MCSF Blount Island, the JEA substation faces the same storm surge exposure, compounded by shoreline loss and water intrusion already threatening electrical systems; rated **Medium** risk but also at **Immediate** urgency given the facility's global crisis-response prepositioning mission. At Naval Air Station Jacksonville, the JEA substation north of the base is rated **Medium** risk at **Immediate** urgency, with lack of redundancy as the primary stressor; JEA has already initiated grid hardening and microgrid feasibility studies in response. At Camp Blanding Joint Training Center, the FPL substation north of the base is rated **High** risk at **Near-Term** urgency, driven by both lack of redundancy and wildfire exposure along the transmission corridor serving Clay County.

**Near-Term Adaptation Strategies (0–5 Years).** The highest-leverage near-term action is expanding the scope of existing JEA IGSAs at NAS Jacksonville and NS Mayport to explicitly include resilience and redundancy provisions — not merely routine O&M. This is an administrative and diplomatic action requiring no new capital and directly addresses the confirmed MSA gap. Simultaneously, accelerating JEA's in-progress microgrid feasibility study at NAS Jacksonville toward a funded project — using ERCIP (Energy Resilience and Conservation Investment Program) or ESPC authorities — would provide islanding capability for Tier 1 loads within the planning horizon. At MCSF Blount Island, the P035 Dual Fuel Generator Project is already in Final Design Authority with ERCIP funding approved; the near-term priority is ensuring it reaches construction without delay, as it will install generators in all nine critical facilities and reduce JEA grid dependency. For NS Mayport, flood barrier deployment or dry-type transformer installation at the Mayport Substation — coordinated with JEA under the expanded IGSA — addresses the highest-urgency risk directly. For Camp Blanding, establishing baseline coordination with FPL and engaging FPSC Storm Hardening reporting requirements will surface planned utility hardening investments and align them with installation needs; prescribed fire programs already in place at Blanding should explicitly include transmission corridor fuel reduction as an objective.

**Long-Term Adaptation Strategies (5–20 Years).** The durable solution across all four installations is breaking the single-substation dependency. A regional utility coordination framework — convening JEA, FPL, Seminole Electric, Clay County Utility Authority, and TECO under a formal resilience-focused MOU — would enable coordinated capital investment planning tied to installation vulnerability timelines. For NS Mayport and MCSF Blount Island, long-term power resilience is inseparable from shoreline resilience: seawall adaptation, living shorelines, and berms protecting substation access corridors are co-benefits of ongoing coastal projects already scoped in the MIRR. At Camp Blanding, the installation's 73,000 acres and rural land availability position it for utility-scale solar development under Enhanced Use Lease (EUL) authority, paired with battery storage, substantially reducing dependence on the FPL distribution corridor and the wildfire risk it carries. At NAS Jacksonville, a dedicated redundant feed from a diverse grid injection point — informed by the JEA microgrid study currently underway — would permanently decouple mission-critical loads from civilian distribution vulnerabilities. All long-term strategies should be incorporated into Installation Energy Plans and coordinated through the MIRR's emerging regional implementation framework.
